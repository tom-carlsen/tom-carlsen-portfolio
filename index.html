The road in

How I got here

01

Platform Expert

I owned deep product knowledge, internal training, and the unofficial title of "person customers call when things get complicated." I learned the product better than almost anyone — and more importantly, I mapped exactly where customers got stuck, frustrated, or lost. That map never left me.

02

Customer Success

A portfolio of accounts, onboarding, QBRs, renewals, and escalations. I learned how to turn technical knowledge into trust. The customers who stayed weren't the ones with the smoothest implementations — they were the ones who believed someone was genuinely in their corner.

03

Team Manager

Hiring, coaching, workload management — making sure what worked for me could scale beyond me. I learned that good CX doesn't scale through willpower. It scales through clarity: clear processes, clear expectations, and a team that knows what "good" looks like without having to ask every time.

04

Project Delivery

Complex implementations, scoping, stakeholder alignment, getting things across the line. I learned that customers don't care about internal constraints — they care whether you show up, communicate clearly, and deliver. That discipline of translating customer need into scoped, executable work changed how I think about everything.

05

Head of Customer Experience Now

The full customer function — strategy, team, process, culture. Making sure every layer works together and that we're building something that gets better over time. This role required everything: the product depth, the relationship instincts, the team-building discipline, the delivery rigor. This is where the arc made sense.

In practice

Work I'm proud of

01

Building processes that make quality the default, not the exception

Quality at scale doesn't come from effort — it comes from structure. Over the years I've built the playbooks, review frameworks, and internal standards that let a team deliver consistently excellent work regardless of who's running a given project. That means defining what "good" looks like concretely, building checkpoints into the workflow, and making it easy to do things right rather than relying on individual judgement every time.

The result is a function where outcomes are predictable. Customers get a consistent experience, the team isn't constantly firefighting, and quality becomes a property of the system — not a personality trait of the person running it.

02

Creative collaboration across industries to bring interactive content to life

Interactive content projects are never purely technical — they're creative partnerships. Working across finance, retail, healthcare, education, and beyond, I've collaborated directly with clients to understand their audience, translate their vision into something the platform could deliver, and push the work to a level they hadn't imagined when they started. Each industry speaks a different language; the discipline is learning it quickly and building something that fits.

Projects that started as vague briefs became polished, effective content. Clients who expected a vendor got a collaborator — and came back for more.

03

Hundreds of project templates that put customers in the driver's seat

Customers shouldn't need us to do their best work. I built out a library of hundreds of project templates — covering a wide range of formats, industries, and use cases — designed so customers could take something polished off the shelf, adapt it to their needs, and publish engaging interactive content to their own audiences independently. The templates encode best practices invisibly: structure, pacing, interaction design, and content hierarchy baked in from the start.

Customers moved faster and produced better work. The team spent less time on repetitive build requests and more time on the work that actually required expertise.

What I believe

How I think about CX

01

Good CX is an ops problem, not a feelings problem.

Customer experience is downstream of process. The teams that consistently deliver great CX aren't just warmer or more empathetic — they have clearer handoffs, better tooling, and a shared definition of what "good" looks like. Caring is table stakes. The structure is what makes it scale.

02

The best customer relationships are built in the hardest moments.

Easy renewals tell you almost nothing. How you show up during a critical bug, a missed deadline, or a failed implementation — that's where trust is made or lost. I've seen more customers become advocates after a difficult moment than after a smooth one, because handling difficulty well is rare and memorable.

03

A CX team is only as good as how well they understand the product.

You cannot advocate for customers if you don't understand what the product can and can't do. The gap between what was sold and what gets delivered lives in that knowledge gap. The best CS reps I've coached weren't the most polished — they were the ones who were genuinely curious about how things worked.
